# Designed Around You
### Day 2 Run of Show: Human-Centered Design Summit
**Non-technical audience. Workshop format. 34 slides plus facilitation blocks.**

Day 1 answered "why does this take so long?"
Day 2 answers "how do we make sure the long thing is the right thing, and how do you shape it?"

---

## BEFORE THE DAY STARTS

### Hard prerequisite: Day 1 capture
Day 2 cannot run without synthesized pain points. Add this to the end of Day 1:

- Each table gets index cards in the final 20 minutes.
- Prompt: "Write the three moments in your week where the tools you use get in your way. One per card. Be specific about the moment, not the tool."
- Collect all cards before anyone leaves.

### Overnight synthesis (2 to 3 people, roughly 2 hours)
1. Cluster the cards by underlying job, not by system named.
2. Pick the four to six clusters with the most weight and the widest spread across tables.
3. Reframe each as a **How Might We** statement. Reframe complaints into invitations.
   - Raw: "The reporting tool is terrible and I don't trust the numbers."
   - Framed: "How might we help someone trust a number without checking it three separate ways?"
4. Write each on a poster. One per table. Include two or three verbatim quotes from the cards underneath so the table sees their own words.
5. Send the framed statements to the engineering leads that evening so they arrive warm, not cold.

### Room setup
- Round tables of 5 to 6. No theater seating. This is a working day.
- Per table: How Might We poster, thick markers (not pens, markers force big simple drawing), sticky notes, blank sketch sheets pre-printed with 8 empty boxes, one large concept poster template, table number tent.
- Wall space cleared for posting concepts. Painter's tape.
- Dot stickers for voting, roughly 3 per person per vote.
- A camera. Photograph every sketch and poster before anything comes off the wall.

### Roles
| Role | Job |
|---|---|
| Host | Runs the room, keeps time, holds the energy |
| Table facilitators (1 per table) | Keeps the table moving, does not contribute ideas |
| Scribe | Captures decisions, questions, and commitments in real time |
| Engineering leads (2 to 4) | Silent observers in the morning, on stage after lunch |

### Concept poster template (pre-print one per table)
> **Name it:**
> **Who is this for:**
> **The job it does:**
> **How it works, in three steps:**
> **What changes for that person:**

# BLOCK 0: WELCOME BACK (15 min)

### Slide 1: Title
**Timing:** 0:30

**Graphics:** A photo of hands, several sets, working over a table covered in sketches and sticky notes. Warm, human, slightly messy. Deliberately the opposite of Day 1's cold iceberg.

**On-slide words:**
> Designed Around You
> Day 2: Human-Centered Design

**Speaker notes:** Welcome them back. Set the tone immediately and physically: yesterday they sat and listened, today they will stand up, draw, and argue. Tell them the day is built around their hands, not yours.

---

### Slide 2: The bridge from Day 1
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** Split slide. Left: the Day 1 iceberg, small and greyed out. Right: a person's silhouette at the center of a circle of arrows. An arrow points from left to right.

**On-slide words:**
> Yesterday: why it takes so long.
> Today: how we make sure it's the right thing.

**Speaker notes:** Do a fast callback so the two days feel like one argument. Yesterday you showed them the hidden work: the iceberg, the questions nobody asks in the meeting, the data, the security, the policy. Then land the turn that makes today matter: if building the right thing is expensive, building the *wrong* thing is catastrophic. Yesterday's closing line was that the most expensive thing you can build is the wrong thing quickly. Today is the discipline that stops that from happening. Say plainly that they are the ones who can prevent it, because they know the work.

---

### Slide 3: What you told us
**Timing:** 9:30

**Graphics:** The four to six How Might We statements laid out cleanly, with two or three verbatim quotes from the index cards under each one, in quotation marks. Their words, not yours.

**On-slide words:**
> You wrote these. We just organized them.

**Speaker notes:** Walk each statement out loud, slowly, reading one real quote under each. This is the single most important trust-building moment of the day, so do not rush it and do not paraphrase their words into corporate language. Two things to say explicitly. First, these came from their cards, and every card is in the pile even if it isn't on the wall. Second, ask directly: did we get these right, and is anything big missing? Take two or three corrections live and write them up. If they see you change a statement because of what they said, you have the room for the rest of the day.

**Facilitator note:** Table facilitators post the matching How Might We poster on their table during this slide.

# BLOCK 1: TECHNOLOGY DESIGNED AROUND HUMANS (55 min)

### Slide 4: Section title
**Timing:** 0:30

**Graphics:** A single chair, well-worn, lit simply. A designed object that exists only because a body exists.

**On-slide words:**
> Technology designed around humans

**Speaker notes:** One line of setup. Everything built for people carries an assumption about those people, and most bad tools are not badly made. They are made for someone who does not exist.

---

### Slide 5: The slow elevator
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** Two panels. Left: an office building with a frustrated crowd waiting, captioned "Make the elevator faster." Right: the same lobby with mirrors installed, people calmly looking at their phones and themselves, captioned "Complaints stopped."

**On-slide words:**
> The problem wasn't the speed. It was the waiting.

**Speaker notes:** Tell the classic story. A building gets complaints that the elevators are too slow. The engineering fix is expensive and slow: new motors, new shafts. Someone reframes the problem instead. People do not hate the wait, they hate the *empty* wait. Mirrors go in. Complaints stop. The elevator was never made faster.

Make the point carefully, because it's the spine of the whole morning: the requested solution was "faster elevators," the actual job was "make this wait tolerable." If you build what was requested you spend a fortune and solve it slowly. If you understand the job, you solve it cheaply. Then say the uncomfortable half out loud: engineers cannot find that reframe alone, and neither can users alone. It only shows up when both are in the room. That is why they are here.

---

### Slide 6: What people ask for vs. what they need
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** Three columns: "What was asked for" / "The job underneath" / "What we might build." One row filled in with the elevator. Two more rows left visibly blank.

**On-slide words:**
> The ask is a clue, not an instruction.

**Speaker notes:** Generalize the elevator into something they can use. When someone asks for a feature, they have already done a private translation from their problem into a solution, usually based on a tool they've seen before. That translation is useful information, but it is not the requirement. Say the thing that gives them permission for the rest of the day: nobody is being accused of asking for the wrong thing. The ask is genuinely valuable, it just isn't the whole story, and the job underneath it is where the good options live.

**Facilitator note:** Optional 3-minute table turn here. Ask each table to fill one blank row with something they asked for recently and what the job underneath actually was. Take two out loud.

---

### Slide 7: So what is human-centered design
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** Three overlapping circles, plain and unfussy: Desirable (do people want it), Feasible (can we build it), Viable (can we sustain it). The sweet spot in the middle is highlighted.

**On-slide words:**
> Start with the person. Not the technology. Not the budget.

**Speaker notes:** Give the plain definition. Human-centered design means starting with a real understanding of the people who will use the thing, and staying with them the whole way through, rather than checking in at the end. The three circles show why it is not just being nice: something can be lovable and unbuildable, or buildable and unwanted. The job is the overlap. Point out that today maps onto exactly these three circles: the morning is desirable, after lunch is feasible, and the backlog session is viable.

---

### Slide 8: The double diamond
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** The double diamond drawn simply and large, with the four phases labeled underneath: Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver. Arrows on the diamonds showing wide, narrow, wide, narrow. Mark clearly where the room is right now: they finished Discover yesterday.

**On-slide words:**
> Go wide. Then narrow. Twice.

**Speaker notes:** This is the one process model they should leave with, so teach it properly.

Diamond one is about the problem. Discover means going wide: gathering everything, all the friction, no filtering, which is exactly what their index cards did yesterday. Define means narrowing to the problem worth solving, which is what the How Might We statements on their tables are.

Diamond two is about the solution. Develop means going wide again with many possible answers, which is the studio they are about to do. Deliver means narrowing to what actually gets built, which is the afternoon.

Name the discipline that people find hardest: the widening steps feel like waste. Generating twelve ideas when you will build one looks inefficient, and the instinct is to skip to the answer. But the quality of the final choice is capped by the quality of the options you generated. You cannot pick a good idea you never had.

---

### Slide 9: Where this usually goes wrong
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** The double diamond again, but with the first diamond crossed out and a straight line drawn from "someone's idea" directly to "build it."

**On-slide words:**
> Most failed projects were built exactly as requested.

**Speaker notes:** The common failure is not bad engineering. It is skipping the first diamond entirely: someone has an idea, it goes straight into a plan, and eighteen months later a technically excellent thing arrives that nobody uses. Be candid here, including about your own organization, because a little self-criticism buys enormous credibility with a skeptical room. Then connect it to yesterday: this is what "the wrong thing quickly" actually looks like in practice.

---

### Slide 10: What this means for you
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** A timeline of a project with the audience's icon appearing at many points along it, not just at the start and the end.

**On-slide words:**
> You are not a stakeholder we check in with. You are part of the process.

**Speaker notes:** This is the "what's in it for me" slide, so make it concrete. Tell them what changes: they will be asked about jobs, not features. They will see rough things early, and rough on purpose, because polished work is hard to criticize honestly. They will be asked to react to something wrong rather than to describe something right, since people are far better at critique than at specification. And they should expect to be asked "why" more than they are used to, which is not doubt, it is the first diamond.

---

### Slide 11: Transition to the studio
**Timing:** 4:00 (plus break)

**Graphics:** The double diamond, with a bright marker circle around the top of diamond two: Develop.

**On-slide words:**
> After the break: you're here.

**Speaker notes:** Point at the diamond and tell them exactly where they are going. The problems are defined and on their tables. After the break, they widen. Set expectations for the anxiety that is about to arrive: they will be asked to draw, most of them will hate that idea, and it will be fine. Send them to break on that note rather than explaining the exercise now. Explaining the studio before a break wastes the explanation.

# BLOCK 2: YOUR TURN TO DESIGN (90 min)

### Slide 12: Section title and the rules
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** Four large icons with short labels: a stick figure drawing badly (no skill required), a clock (fast beats good), a stack of paper (quantity first), a crossed-out red pen (no critique yet).

**On-slide words:**
> No drawing skill required. Boxes and stick figures win.

**Speaker notes:** Lower the barrier hard and fast, because half the room is quietly panicking about being asked to draw. Say it directly: nobody is judging the art, and the ugliest sketch in the room has won plenty of these before. Give the four rules. Quantity before quality, because you cannot pick a good idea you never had. Fast beats polished. Words on a sketch are allowed and encouraged. And no critique during generation, not even of your own idea, since the fastest way to kill a room's ideas is to evaluate them while they are still arriving.

---

### Slide 13: Warm-up
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A sheet divided into 8 boxes, with a large timer graphic. One example box sketched crudely on purpose, with visible bad handwriting.

**On-slide words:**
> 8 boxes. 4 minutes. Go.
> "A better way to wait in line."

**Speaker notes:** Run a throwaway warm-up on a neutral prompt so the first sketch of the day carries no stakes. Eight boxes, four minutes, one idea per box, silent. Push them when they stall around box four, which everyone does, because the interesting ideas live after the obvious ones run out. When time is up, do not share them. Just ask for a show of hands: who filled all eight, and who surprised themselves. Then say the honest thing, that boxes six through eight were the strange ones and the strange ones are the point.

**Facilitator note:** Table facilitators sketch too. Visibly. Badly.

---

### Slide 14: The brief
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** A clean layout of the How Might We statement placement, showing each table which statement is theirs. Include the quotes again.

**On-slide words:**
> Your table. Your problem. Your turn.

**Speaker notes:** Assign each table its How Might We statement, which is already on their poster. Tell them the one constraint that matters: design for the person in the quote, not for the whole organization. Specific beats universal every time, and a solution that perfectly serves one real person usually serves many, while a solution designed for everyone serves nobody. Remind them they are still in the wide part of the diamond, so nobody should be converging yet.

---

### Slide 15: Round 1, silent sketching
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** Large timer. Minimal text. The word SILENT prominently displayed.

**On-slide words:**
> 12 minutes. Silent. Individual.
> As many ideas as you can.

**Speaker notes:** Explain why silent, because people find it strange: talking first means the loudest and most senior voice anchors the whole table, and you lose every idea that disagreed with them. Silence protects the quiet people, and the quiet people are frequently the ones doing the actual work being discussed. Everyone sketches alone. Words allowed. No talking, including facilitators.

**Facilitator note:** Enforce the silence gently but firmly. Give a warning at 6 minutes and at 2 minutes. Watch for anyone frozen and put a marker in their hand with a whisper of "just draw the annoying part."

---

### Slide 16: Round 2, share and steal
**Timing:** 15:00

**Graphics:** A table icon with arrows circulating between seats, and a hand placing a dot sticker.

**On-slide words:**
> 60 seconds each. Then vote with dots.
> Stealing is encouraged.

**Speaker notes:** Each person gets 60 seconds to walk their table through their sketches, with no defending and no apologizing, which people will try to do constantly. Others may ask clarifying questions only, not "have you considered." Then each person places three dots on the ideas or fragments of ideas anywhere on the table that feel strongest, including their own. Say clearly that combining ideas is the goal, not choosing a winner, and that the best concept usually turns out to be a mash-up of three people's fragments.

**Facilitator note:** Keep to 60 seconds per person hard. A quiet timer at each table helps more than the host calling time.

---

### Slide 17: Round 3, build one concept
**Timing:** 25:00

**Graphics:** The concept poster template, blown up large and filled in with a rough worked example so nobody has to guess what "good" looks like.

**On-slide words:**
> One concept per table. Fill the poster.

**Speaker notes:** Now they converge for the first time. Each table builds one concept, drawing from the dotted ideas, and fills the poster: name it, who it is for, the job it does, how it works in three steps, and what changes for that person. Stress the last line hardest, because "what changes for that person" is what makes it a design instead of a feature list, and it is the line the afternoon will lean on. Tell them the name matters more than they think, since a concept with a name survives a meeting and a concept without one does not.

**Facilitator note:** At 15 minutes remaining, warn tables still arguing about scope to pick one thing and go deep rather than covering everything shallowly. Photograph all individual sketches now while tables work, so nothing is lost.

---

### Slide 18: Share out
**Timing:** 23:00

**Graphics:** Simple. A timer and the running order of tables.

**On-slide words:**
> 3 minutes per table. Then we look for patterns.

**Speaker notes:** Each table posts their concept on the wall and presents for three minutes. Hold the time strictly and visibly, so later tables get the same airtime as the first.

Do not open the floor to critique after each one. Instead, ask the room for one question per concept, questions only, and hold everything else. After all tables have gone, spend the last 5 minutes on patterns rather than judgment: which concepts serve the same underlying job, where two tables independently landed in the same place, and which How Might We statements produced the most energy. Independent convergence is a strong signal and worth naming out loud when it happens.

Close the block by telling them exactly what happens over lunch: the engineering leads will study these posters, and they will come back with honest answers rather than polite ones.

**Facilitator note:** Scribe captures every concept name and its one-line job description into a running list. Engineering leads receive that list plus photographs at the start of lunch.

---

## LUNCH (60 min)
**Engineering leads working session.** Leads review every poster on the wall with the scribe's list in hand. For each concept they prepare: one honest sentence on what is straightforward, one on what is hard and why, and one open question they need answered before they could size it. They are not preparing verdicts. They are preparing an honest briefing.

# BLOCK 3: THE REALITY CHECK (60 min)

### Slide 19: Section title and the frame
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** Two people looking at the same poster from either side, both pointing at it. Deliberately not a judge and a defendant.

**On-slide words:**
> From concept to reality check

**Speaker notes:** Frame this session very deliberately, because the room is bracing to have their morning's work marked. Say plainly that nothing here is a verdict, and that no idea is being killed in this session. The goal is shared understanding: they know the job, engineering knows the terrain, and neither knows the whole map alone. Remind them of yesterday's material, since this is where the iceberg, the data, the security, and the policy come back and land on their own ideas. That is the moment the two days click together.

---

### Slide 20: The three questions
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** Three numbered cards, large and clean.

**On-slide words:**
> 1. Could we build it?
> 2. What would it take?
> 3. What would we need to figure out first?

**Speaker notes:** Introduce the three questions and be explicit about the one that is missing. Engineering answers these three, and the room will notice that "should we build it?" is not on the list. Tell them why: that question is theirs, not engineering's, and they will answer it themselves in the next session. Engineering's job is to tell them what a thing costs and what is unknown. Deciding what is worth it belongs to the people who know the mission. Saying this out loud transfers ownership and changes how the whole afternoon feels.

---

### Slide 21: How we'll talk about difficulty
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** Three bands, not a traffic light. Well-trodden path / Some unknowns / Genuine unknowns. Under each, a plain-language description of what it means for time and confidence.

**On-slide words:**
> "Hard" usually means "uncertain," not "impossible."

**Speaker notes:** Give them shared vocabulary before the feedback starts, or every comment will be heard as yes or no. Well-trodden means the team has done this shape of thing before and can estimate it with confidence. Some unknowns means it is buildable but there are open questions that change the size. Genuine unknowns means real investigation is needed before anyone can honestly estimate.

Make the reframe explicit: when an engineer says something is hard, they usually do not mean impossible, they mean uncertain, and uncertainty is what makes estimates wide. That connects straight back to yesterday's "ask for ranges, not dates."

---

### Slide 22: Live feasibility, concept by concept
**Timing:** 40:00

**Graphics:** Minimal. The three questions small in the corner, and space for the concept being discussed. The posters on the wall are the real visual here.

**On-slide words:**
> [Concept name]

**Speaker notes:** Work through each concept in turn. With six tables this is roughly 6 to 7 minutes each, so hold the clock.

For each one: the table gives a 30-second reminder of the job it does. An engineering lead answers the three questions honestly, using the vocabulary from the previous slide. Then two minutes of open exchange, where the most valuable move is engineering asking "what if we solved it this way instead?" and the table saying whether that would still do the job. Some of the best outcomes of the day come from exactly that trade.

Coach the leads beforehand on two habits. Always say what *is* easy before what is hard, because starting with the obstacle reads as a no. And when something is genuinely difficult, explain which of yesterday's forces is causing it, whether that is the data, the security, the policy, or a dependency, since the room now has the vocabulary to understand the answer.

**Facilitator note:** Scribe fills a card per concept in real time: difficulty band, the single biggest unknown, and any alternative approach suggested. These cards drive the next session, so they must be done before the break.

---

### Slide 23: What we just learned
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** The concepts listed with their difficulty bands attached. First time the room sees the full picture together.

**On-slide words:**
> Nothing was killed. Everything got clearer.

**Speaker notes:** Summarize the shape of what emerged, not each individual answer. Usually a few patterns are worth naming: several concepts share one underlying dependency, or the hardest parts cluster around data quality or permissions rather than the visible feature. Say clearly that no idea was eliminated, and that the room now knows something it did not know this morning, which is what each idea would actually cost. Send them to break with the next question already planted: now that you know the price of each, which ones are worth it?

# BLOCK 4: THE OPPORTUNITY BACKLOG (60 min)

### Slide 24: Section title
**Timing:** 3:00

**Graphics:** A wall of concept cards being sorted by hands into groups. Motion, not a finished chart.

**On-slide words:**
> What gets built first, and why

**Speaker notes:** Open by handing them the question you withheld earlier. Engineering answered "could we" and "what would it take." Now the room answers "should we," and they own it. Be honest about the constraint that makes the session necessary: everything on that wall could be built, and not everything can be built at once, so sequence is the real decision.

---

### Slide 25: How we'll decide
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A 2x2 grid. Vertical axis: "How much this changes your work" (low to high). Horizontal axis: "What it would take" (light to heavy). Quadrants labeled in plain language: **Do now**, **Plan carefully**, **Easy wins**, **Not now**.

**On-slide words:**
> You own the vertical. Engineering owns the horizontal.

**Speaker notes:** Explain the grid and, more importantly, who owns which axis. The vertical axis is impact on their actual work, and only they can place that. The horizontal is effort and uncertainty, and engineering already placed it during the reality check. Neither side can fill in the other's axis, which is the whole reason both are in the room.

Walk the quadrants briefly. High impact and light effort are the easy wins that should happen soon. High impact and heavy effort are the ones that need real planning rather than optimism. Low impact and light effort are pleasant but should not crowd out the first two. Low impact and heavy effort are not now.

Be precise about "not now," because this is where rooms get bruised: it means not in this cycle, with the reasons recorded, and it can change when circumstances do. It does not mean the idea was bad, and it does not mean the idea disappears.

---

### Slide 26: Place the concepts
**Timing:** 20:00

**Graphics:** The empty 2x2, printed large on the wall as a physical grid. Concept cards ready to be placed.

**On-slide words:**
> Place them. Then argue about it.

**Speaker notes:** Run this standing up at the wall, since sitting kills the energy of the last working session of a two-day summit. Engineering places each concept card horizontally first, using the difficulty bands from the reality check. Then the room places each card vertically by impact.

The argument that happens while placing is the actual output, so do not rush past disagreement. When two people place the same card very differently, ask both to say who they were thinking about, because they are usually both right about different people, and that difference is real information. Have the scribe capture those disagreements rather than resolving them away.

**Facilitator note:** Timebox debate on any single card to 2 minutes. If unresolved, place it on the line and move on. Scribe records the split.

---

### Slide 27: The shortlist
**Timing:** 15:00

**Graphics:** The completed 2x2, with three to five concepts circled in the upper region.

**On-slide words:**
> The three to five we take forward first.

**Speaker notes:** Narrow to a shortlist of three to five, which is the second diamond closing. Resist a longer list even under pressure, and say why: a prioritized list of fifteen is not prioritized, it is a wish list with numbers on it, and everything on it moves slowly.

For each shortlisted item, state three things aloud so the room hears the commitment take shape: the job it serves, the biggest unknown from the reality check, and what the immediate next step is. Then be straight about what this is and is not. It is a shared recommendation with real evidence behind it. It is not yet a funded, dated plan, and pretending otherwise would break the trust built over two days.

---

### Slide 28: Where everything else goes
**Timing:** 10:00

**Graphics:** The full wall photographed, with every concept visible, and a simple label showing all of it is being captured into the opportunity backlog.

**On-slide words:**
> Nothing gets thrown away.

**Speaker notes:** This slide protects everyone whose idea did not make the shortlist, which is most of the room. Everything on the wall goes into the opportunity backlog with its difficulty band, its unknowns, and the reasoning from today attached. Explain what the record is worth: when circumstances change, when a dependency lands, or when someone asks for something similar next quarter, the work already exists rather than starting over. Say the sentence directly, that no idea from today disappears, and mean it, because they will check.

---

### Slide 29: What changed today
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A simple before and after. Before: a list of complaints. After: framed problems, concepts, difficulty, sequence.

**On-slide words:**
> This morning: pain points. Now: a plan with evidence.

**Speaker notes:** Show them the distance travelled in one day, because in the middle of a workshop people lose track of it. They arrived with friction they lived with privately. They now have framed problems, concepts they made themselves, honest feasibility from the people who would build them, and an agreed sequence. Name the thing that makes it different from a normal wish list: every item carries both sides of the story, the job and the cost, which is exactly what usually goes missing.

---

# BLOCK 5: WHAT HAPPENS NEXT, AND CLOSING (40 min)

### Slide 30: Section title
**Timing:** 2:00

**Graphics:** A calendar with specific dates visible, not a generic roadmap arrow. Concreteness is the point.

**On-slide words:**
> What happens next

**Speaker notes:** Tell them this is the part that determines whether the summit mattered. Everyone in the room has been to a workshop that produced nothing, and they are quietly waiting to find out if this was one. Answer that directly.

---

### Slide 31: The commitments
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** A table with three columns: What / Who (a named person, not a team) / By when (a real date). Filled in live or pre-filled and confirmed.

**On-slide words:**
> Every commitment has a name and a date.

**Speaker notes:** Go line by line. Names of people, not team names, and real dates, not "in the coming weeks." At minimum cover: who writes up the day and when they will circulate it, who owns each shortlisted concept, when the feasibility unknowns get investigated, and when this group hears back next.

If something is genuinely uncertain, commit to a date for the decision rather than inventing a date for the outcome. That distinction is both honest and useful, and this audience will respect it more than optimism.

---

### Slide 32: How you stay involved
**Timing:** 10:00

**Graphics:** A loop, not a line. Points on the loop: early rough concepts, feedback sessions, testing with real users, then back around.

**On-slide words:**
> This isn't the last time we ask.

**Speaker notes:** Set expectations for the ongoing relationship, since one summit is not human-centered design, it is an event. Tell them concretely what to expect: they will be shown rough work early and asked to react, some of them will be asked to be tested with rather than presented to, and the loop repeats rather than ending at a launch.

Ask for something specific in return, because a request lands better than a vague invitation. Ask who is willing to be contacted for 30 minutes when a rough version exists, and get hands or names in the room while the energy is still there.

---

### Slide 33: The closing
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** The Day 1 iceberg and today's photo of hands over sketches, side by side, finally in one frame.

**On-slide words:**
> We can build almost anything.
> You're how we know what to build.

**Speaker notes:** Bring the two days together in a short close, and keep it short, because a long ending undoes a good day.

Day 1 was honest about the cost of building: the hidden work, the data, the security, the policy, the reason a small ask becomes a long project. Day 2 was about making sure that cost buys something worth having. Reflect back one or two specific things the room produced today, by name, since specificity is what makes gratitude credible.

Then land the closing pair. Yesterday ended on the most expensive thing you can build being the wrong thing quickly. Today ends on its answer: they are the reason it will be the right thing. Thank them by name where you can, and finish clean.

---

### Slide 34: Contact and close
**Timing:** 2:00

**Graphics:** Simple. Names and contact details for the people who own the commitments. Optionally a photo of the concept wall from today.
