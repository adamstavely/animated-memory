# Where We Actually Are
### Day 1 Run of Show: Summit
**Non-technical audience. Mixed keynote, talk, and working session. 6 blocks.**

Day 1 answers: here's your reality, here's ours, here's the honest cost of things, and here's what we should be solving.
Day 2 answers: here's how we choose together, and here's your part in it.

**The single most important output of Day 1 is Block 6.** Everything before it is either building the credibility to run that session, or feeding it raw material. If Block 6 works, Day 2 has a validated design brief and no overnight scramble. If it does not, Day 2 is starting from guesswork.

---

## WHAT CHANGED FROM THE ORIGINAL AGENDA

| Original | Now | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Application training (session 6) | Moved out to post-summit clinics | Cannot teach hands-on skills to a ballroom in 55 minutes. Also gives the summit a concrete afterlife. |
| Operational effectiveness (session 5) | Merged with the useful remainder of training | Same session wearing two hats. |
| Tales from the front line | Restructured to capture, not just perform | Stage stories produce anecdotes. This version produces a usable corpus for Day 2. |
| No closing working session | New Block 6: naming the problems worth solving | Kills the fragile overnight synthesis. Ends the day on the audience's own words. |
| AI roadmap | Reframed as building blocks, explicitly resequenceable | A fixed roadmap makes Day 2's studio theater, and the room will know. |

## ROOM SETUP AND MATERIALS

- Round tables of 5 to 6. Not theater seating, even for the keynote. The room needs to be able to work at any moment.
- **The friction wall.** A long clear wall, visible all day, empty at the start. This is the spine of the day.
- Per table: stack of index cards (plan on 10 per person), thick markers, table number tent.
- Painter's tape, dot stickers, large sticky notes for cluster headers.
- Blank posters for the How Might We statements in Block 6.
- A camera. Photograph the wall at the end of every block so you have a record of how it grew.

### Roles
| Role | Job |
|---|---|
| Host | Runs the room, holds time and energy across the day |
| Table facilitators (1 per table) | Keep tables moving, do not contribute content |
| Scribe | Captures decisions, questions, and commitments in real time |
| Wall keeper | Owns the friction wall all day: posts cards, keeps it legible, photographs it |
| Storytellers (3 to 4) | Prepped in advance for Block 2 |
| Engineering leads | Present all day, visible, listening. Speaking in Block 3 and on Day 2. |

# BLOCK 1: WELCOME AND OPENING KEYNOTE (50 min)
### "Making technology work for you"

### Slide 1: Title
**Timing:** 0:30

**Graphics:** A person at a desk doing real work, technology visible but peripheral, not the subject of the photo. Deliberately not a glowing circuit board or a robot hand.

**On-slide words:**
> Making Technology Work for You
> Day 1: Where we actually are

**Speaker notes:** Welcome them. Keep the housekeeping to under a minute and get to the substance fast, because the first five minutes set whether this reads as a real conversation or a corporate showcase.

---

### Slide 2: Why you're in the room
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** Simple. A single line of text on a plain background. Resist the urge to decorate this one.

**On-slide words:**
> We build things for you. We don't always get it right.

**Speaker notes:** This is the most important five minutes of the summit, because it establishes whether anyone believes the next two days.

Open with candor, not with achievements. Name specifically, without hedging, at least one thing that has not worked well, ideally something the room already grumbles about. Do not follow it with an excuse. The room is braced for a defense, and not delivering one is what earns you the rest of the day.

Then say why they are here in plain terms. They were invited because they do the work, and the gap between something being delivered and something being useful is a gap only they can see. Be explicit that this is not a listening session in the ceremonial sense. By the end of tomorrow afternoon there will be a shortlist with names and dates attached to it.

Avoid: a highlight reel, a list of things shipped last year, or any version of "we've come a long way." Save all of it. If they want it, they will ask.

---

### Slide 3: The reframe
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** Two panels. Left: a person contorted awkwardly around a rigid machine, captioned "How it usually goes." Right: the machine bent around the person, captioned "How it should go." Slightly humorous is fine here.

**On-slide words:**
> Technology should adapt to the work.
> Too often the work adapts to the technology.

**Speaker notes:** Give them the idea that the whole summit hangs on.

Every tool carries an assumption about who is using it and how they work. When that assumption is wrong, people do not stop working, they absorb the difference: workarounds, second copies of data, a spreadsheet on the side, a step everyone knows to skip. That absorbed cost is invisible from where you sit, because from the outside the system looks like it is working.

Ask for a show of hands on something concrete: who keeps a personal spreadsheet or document because the official system does not do what they need? The hands going up is the point, and it is worth more than any slide. Name what those hands mean: every one of them is a design failure that someone has been quietly paying for.

Land the sentence that carries into tomorrow: the goal of these two days is to move that cost off them and onto the systems, which is where it belongs.

---

### Slide 4: What these two days are
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** A simple two-column map of both days, each block named in one plain phrase. Show the whole journey at once, including tomorrow.

**On-slide words:**
> Day 1: What's real. Day 2: What's next.

**Speaker notes:** Walk the map so nobody spends the day wondering where this is going. Today is about reality: their friction, the honest mechanics of how this work happens, what the technology can and cannot do right now, and then a working session where the room names the problems worth solving. Tomorrow they design against those problems, get honest feasibility from the people who would build it, and help set the sequence.

Say clearly what will be different about tomorrow: they will be working, not watching, and they should come ready for that.

Give them one warning that pays off later. There is a session before lunch about why building software takes as long as it does, and it will be more honest than they expect. Ask them to hold their skepticism until then rather than during it.

---

### Slide 5: The friction wall
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A photo of the actual empty wall in the actual room, with a few example cards shown at large size so the format is unmistakable. Show a good card and a weak card side by side.

**On-slide words:**
> One moment per card. Be specific.

**Speaker notes:** Introduce the mechanism that runs all day, and teach the format now so you do not have to reteach it in every block.

Cards are on every table. Any time today something reminds them of a moment where the tools got in the way, they write it and post it. During sessions, at breaks, whenever. The wall is theirs, and it will be full by the afternoon.

Teach specificity by contrast, using the two examples on the slide. A weak card names a system and a feeling: "the reporting tool is terrible." A strong card names a moment: "Tuesday, needed last month's totals for a brief, three systems gave three different numbers, spent 40 minutes reconciling by hand." Say why the difference matters: the first one cannot be designed against, and the second one practically designs itself.

Tell them where it goes, because people write more when they know it is not disappearing into a box. Everything on that wall gets read out this afternoon, and the room will sort it together in the last session.

---

### Slide 6: What we're asking of you
**Timing:** 21:00 remaining, including keynote body and transition

**Graphics:** Three plain icons: an open mouth (say the awkward thing), a hand (make things), a calendar (stay involved after).

**On-slide words:**
> Be blunt. Be specific. Stay with us after today.

**Speaker notes:** This is the body of the keynote, and the speaker should fill it with their own material. Structure it against these three asks so it lands somewhere useful.

Be blunt. Give explicit permission for criticism, and mean it. If leadership is in the room, have them say it, because permission from anyone junior is worthless. The failure mode of a summit like this is politeness, and polite input produces the wrong roadmap.

Be specific. Generalities cannot be built against. "Make it faster" is a feeling, and "this screen takes six clicks to do the thing I do forty times a day" is a design brief.

Stay involved. Two days does not make a system human-centered. What makes the difference is being available when someone shows them something rough and half-finished in three months.

Close the keynote on the promise, and be careful to promise only what you can deliver. Not that everything they raise gets built, because that is a lie and they will know it. Instead: everything they raise gets heard, recorded, and answered honestly, including when the answer is not now and why.

**Facilitator note:** Cards and markers should already be on tables. Wall keeper takes position during the break.

# BLOCK 2: TALES FROM THE FRONT LINE (60 min)

**Purpose:** This session has two jobs, and the second one matters more. Job one is catharsis and shared recognition. Job two is producing a specific, usable corpus of friction for Block 6. The staged stories exist mainly to model the level of specificity you want on the cards, because people imitate what they just watched. Vague stage stories produce a wall of vague cards, and then Block 6 has nothing to work with.

### Slide 7: Section title
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** A microphone on a stand in an ordinary workspace, not a stage. The point is that these are colleagues, not speakers.

**On-slide words:**
> Tales from the front line

**Speaker notes:** Frame the session honestly. The next hour is for the parts of the job nobody puts in a status report. Name the two ground rules that keep it useful. First, no naming and blaming individuals, since the target is systems and processes rather than people. Second, specificity over summary: they should tell about one time, not about how things generally are.

Say directly that engineering leads are in the room and listening, and are not here to respond or defend. That single sentence changes what people are willing to say.

---

### Slide 8 to 11: The staged stories
**Timing:** 24:00 total, roughly 6:00 each

**Graphics:** One slide per storyteller. A photo of them and their role. No bullet points, no summary of the story. Let them talk.

**On-slide words:**
> [Name]
> [Role]

**Speaker notes:** Three or four colleagues, prepped in advance, each telling one specific story about a moment where the tools got in the way. Recruit them from different parts of the organization and different seniority levels, and make sure at least one is very junior, since the junior stories are usually the most concrete and the least filtered.

Brief each storyteller against a tight structure, because unprepped people default to general complaint: what were you trying to do, what happened, what did you do to get around it, and what did that cost you in time or accuracy or frustration. The workaround is the most valuable part, so tell them not to skip it out of embarrassment. Workarounds are inventions, and they are evidence of exactly where the system stopped fitting.

Hold each to six minutes. Applaud each one.

**Facilitator note:** Wall keeper writes a card for each story as it is told and posts it live, so the room sees the wall begin to fill and sees the card format modeled by someone who knows how.

---

### Slide 12: Your turn, on cards
**Timing:** 16:00

**Graphics:** The card format again, large. A timer. The strong and weak examples repeated, since people forget.

**On-slide words:**
> Three cards each. One moment per card.
> When was the last time? What did it cost you?

**Speaker notes:** Now the room writes. Silent, individual, ten minutes, minimum three cards each. Give the prompt that produces specificity: think about the last time, not the usual time. Memory of a specific instance carries detail that general impressions do not.

Push past the first easy card, because the first is always the obvious institutional complaint everybody shares, and the second and third are where the real texture lives.

After ten minutes, tables spend five minutes reading each other's cards aloud briefly and marking any where two people at the table clearly wrote about the same underlying thing. Then everyone posts everything to the wall. Every card goes up, including duplicates, since duplication is signal and Block 6 uses it.

**Facilitator note:** Table facilitators watch for anyone who has written nothing and quietly prompt them with "what slowed you down last week?" Wall keeper starts loose spatial grouping as cards arrive, without labeling anything yet.

---

### Slide 13: Open floor
**Timing:** 16:00

**Graphics:** Blank, or the growing wall on camera if you can put a live shot up.

**On-slide words:**
> Anyone else?

**Speaker notes:** Open the microphone for volunteers, three to five people, two minutes each. This is where the unplanned material arrives, and it is often the sharpest of the day because the staged stories gave people permission.

Manage two risks. If it turns into a pile-on about one system, redirect by asking for a story from a different part of the work. If nobody volunteers, have one or two plants ready, briefed in advance, and do not let the silence sit.

Close the block by pointing at the wall and naming what is now on it. Do not summarize the themes, since that is this afternoon's job and doing it now steals the session's thunder. Just note how much is up there and tell them it grows all day.

# BLOCK 3: PEELING BACK THE CURTAIN (50 min)
### The existing 21-slide deck, inserted here

Run the deck from the earlier run of show, "Why It Takes So Long," essentially as built. It is timed at roughly 40 minutes of content plus 5 minutes of Q&A, which fits this slot with a few minutes of margin.

**Three adjustments now that it sits after Block 2:**

1. **Replace the opening scene with a real one from this morning.** Slide 2 currently opens on a generic "can you just add a button" moment. Open it instead on an actual story told an hour ago, by name, with that person's permission. "This morning [name] told us about spending forty minutes reconciling three numbers. I want to show you what would be involved in fixing that." The talk gets dramatically stronger when the example belongs to the room.

2. **Use the wall as the running example instead of the spreadsheet-to-dashboard hypothetical.** Slides 8, 10, 12, and 14 currently carry the invented example. If a card on the wall is close enough in shape, swap it in. Same structure, same beats, but the audience watches their own friction pick up time from data, then security, then policy. If nothing on the wall fits cleanly, keep the original example rather than forcing it.

3. **Have an engineering lead deliver the three pillar sections.** Slides 9 through 14. This is the first time the room hears directly from someone who builds, and it sets up Day 2's reality check, where those same people give live feasibility feedback. Familiarity built here pays off tomorrow afternoon.

**One caution for the speaker.** Coming directly after an hour of people describing their pain, this talk can land as an excuse if the tone slips. Guard against it explicitly. Say near the top that nothing in this session is a reason why their problems cannot be solved, it is an explanation of what solving them involves, which is different. The closing line does the same work: the most expensive thing you can build is the wrong thing quickly, and that is exactly why the room spent the morning telling you what is actually wrong.

**Facilitator note:** Cards keep flowing during this session. People frequently write their sharpest cards during this talk, because it gives them vocabulary for friction they could not previously name. Make sure markers are within reach and the wall keeper is visibly available.

# BLOCK 4: THE AI ROADMAP, AS BUILDING BLOCKS (55 min)

**Purpose and the trap.** This session immediately follows a morning of people describing daily pain. Present a polished future roadmap now and the room hears: you are building *that* while I still cannot do *this*. Frame it as building blocks and possibilities, keep it grounded in the jobs from the wall, and be explicit that sequence is genuinely open. If the roadmap is presented as fixed, tomorrow's design studio is theater and this audience is smart enough to know it.

### Slide 14: Section title and the ground rules
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** Children's building blocks, or modular components, rather than a rocket, a brain, or a glowing neural network. The metaphor is the message.

**On-slide words:**
> Building blocks, not promises.

**Speaker notes:** Set the rules of the session before any content. Three things.

This is capability, not commitment. Everything shown is a block that could be assembled into something useful, and what gets assembled first is partly what the room decides tomorrow.

Some of this will be resequenced based on the next two days, and say that as a real statement rather than a courtesy. Then honor it tomorrow.

Nothing here replaces the honest conversation from before lunch. Every one of these blocks still carries the data, security, and policy weight they just learned about.

---

### Slide 15: What's here now
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** Two or three capabilities that exist today, each shown through a job it does rather than a technology name. Screenshots of real use beat architecture diagrams every time for this audience.

**On-slide words:**
> Available today

**Speaker notes:** Content is yours, but hold to one discipline: lead every item with the job, not the technology. Not "we have a retrieval-augmented search capability," but "you can ask a question in plain language across these documents and get an answer with the source attached."

For each, say plainly who has access today, what it is genuinely good at, and where it currently falls down. That last part is what makes the rest of the session believable.

Where something connects to a card on the wall, point at the card. This is the strongest move available in this session, and it should happen at least twice.

---

### Slide 16: What's coming next
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** Same format as the previous slide, jobs rather than technologies. Add a rough time horizon per item, expressed as a range rather than a date, which quietly reinforces the morning's lesson.

**On-slide words:**
> Coming next

**Speaker notes:** Same discipline. Jobs first, honest limits attached.

Use ranges rather than dates, and call attention to the fact that you are doing so. The room learned this morning why single dates are usually fiction, so using ranges here proves the talk was not just rhetoric.

For anything genuinely uncertain, say which of the morning's forces is driving the uncertainty. "This one waits on an approval we do not control" is a sentence this audience can now fully understand, and being able to say it is one of the day's real payoffs.

---

### Slide 17: What we're exploring
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** Deliberately rougher and sketchier than the previous two slides. Visual style should communicate uncertainty before anyone reads a word.

**On-slide words:**
> Exploring. No promises.

**Speaker notes:** Keep this short and clearly caveated. Say plainly that some of this will not happen, and that showing it is about direction rather than commitment.

The reason to include it at all is that occasionally someone in the room says "that one, that would change my week," and that is worth knowing early.

---

### Slide 18: What AI won't fix
**Timing:** 10:00

**Graphics:** A plain slide. A short list of honest limitations in plain language.

**On-slide words:**
> The things it isn't good at.

**Speaker notes:** This is the credibility slide of the session, and it buys more trust than everything before it combined. Every audience has sat through AI presentations that promised everything, and they are waiting to find out whether this is another one.

Be concrete about limits that matter to their actual work: where it needs a human to verify, where it is confidently wrong, where the underlying data is not good enough for it to help, and where policy prevents its use regardless of capability.

Say the structural point clearly: AI does not fix broken data, it inherits it, and several of the cards on that wall are data problems wearing an interface costume. That connects the morning's data pillar to this session and sets up tomorrow honestly.

---

### Slide 19: Your turn to shape it
**Timing:** 7:00

**Graphics:** The wall, photographed as it stands now, next to the roadmap items. Two things side by side, not yet connected.

**On-slide words:**
> Which of these matters to what's on that wall?

**Speaker notes:** Close by handing it back. Take a small number of live reactions, and ask a sharper question than "any questions": which of these would actually change something on that wall, and what is on the wall that none of these touches?

The second half of that question is the valuable one. Gaps between the roadmap and the wall are exactly what tomorrow exists to surface, so capture them visibly and thank whoever names one.

**Facilitator note:** Scribe captures every gap named. These go to the Block 6 facilitators before that session starts.

# BLOCK 5: GETTING MORE FROM WHAT YOU ALREADY HAVE (55 min)

**Purpose.** The merged operational effectiveness and training session. The frame that makes it work: before we talk about building anything new, some of what is on that wall already has an answer today, and nobody told you. Positioned this way it reads as service rather than as a lecture about using tools properly.

### Slide 20: Section title
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** The friction wall with three or four cards visibly circled or flagged.

**On-slide words:**
> Some of this has an answer today.

**Speaker notes:** Frame it carefully so it does not sound like blame. The message is not that they should have known, it is that the organization did not tell them well enough, which is a failure on the delivery side and not on theirs.

Set the expectation that this session is deliberately narrow. A handful of things, done properly, rather than a tour of every feature.

---

### Slide 21 to 23: The workflows
**Timing:** 30:00 total, roughly 10:00 each

**Graphics:** One slide per workflow. Show the before and after as a step count or a time saving. Live demo where possible, recorded demo as backup, and never a screenshot walkthrough if either alternative exists.

**On-slide words:**
> [Workflow name]
> Before: [n steps]. After: [n steps].

**Speaker notes:** Pick three workflows using one rule: each must map to a card on the wall from this morning. Say which card as you start each one. That connection is the entire reason this session lands, and without it this is a training session in disguise.

For each, show the current painful path first, briefly, then the better path. Be honest about setup cost if there is one, and about who it does not work for.

Keep the pace up. Three things demonstrated well beats eight things listed.

**Facilitator note:** Have a one-page take-home guide per workflow on the tables. People cannot follow demos and take notes at the same time, and the guide is what makes this stick after they leave.

---

### Slide 24: The clinics
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** A calendar with actual clinic dates on it. Small groups pictured, hands on keyboards, not a classroom.

**On-slide words:**
> Small groups. Hands on. Starting [date].

**Speaker notes:** This is where the old training session went, and it is a better home for it. Say why plainly: you cannot learn to use a tool by watching someone else use it in a ballroom, so instead of pretending, the organization is running hands-on clinics in small groups over the following weeks.

Give real dates, real sizes, real signup, and a named owner. Vagueness here undoes the credibility earned all day.

If people can sign up in the room, have them do it now rather than later. Sign-up rates fall off a cliff once people leave.

---

### Slide 25: The honest limit
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** The friction wall in full, with the three flagged cards now marked as addressed, and the rest of the wall untouched and dominant.

**On-slide words:**
> That covered three cards. Look at the rest of the wall.

**Speaker notes:** This slide protects the integrity of the whole day, so do not skip it under time pressure.

Say it directly: better use of existing tools solved a small fraction of what is on that wall, and the rest is real and requires actual building. Naming that yourself, before anyone in the audience does, is what prevents the session from reading as deflection.

Then hand straight into the last block, which is the point the whole day has been building toward. The wall is full. Now the room decides what actually matters.

# BLOCK 6: NAMING THE PROBLEMS WORTH SOLVING (60 min)

**Purpose.** This is the session that makes Day 2 possible, and it replaces the overnight synthesis I would otherwise be worried about. The room clusters its own friction and frames its own problem statements. Facilitators run the process and hold the discipline, but the words belong to the audience. When they walk into Day 2 tomorrow and see those statements on the tables, they will recognize them as their own, and that recognition is what makes the design studio produce real work instead of polite work.

### Slide 26: Section title
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** The full wall, photographed just now, dense with cards.

**On-slide words:**
> You wrote all of this today.

**Speaker notes:** Open by naming the size of what they produced. Give the actual card count out loud, because a number lands harder than an impression.

Set the task in one sentence: over the next hour the room turns a wall of individual friction into a small number of problems worth solving, and those become tomorrow's agenda.

Set the standard for what a good outcome looks like, because people default to categorizing by system. The goal is not to sort by which tool was named. It is to find the jobs underneath, since the same job shows up across five different systems and that is precisely the pattern worth building against.

---

### Slide 27: How to group them
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A worked example. Four cards that name four different systems, shown grouped under one job. Make the wrong grouping visible too, crossed out: the same four cards sorted by system name into four useless piles of one.

**On-slide words:**
> Group by the job. Not by the system.

**Speaker notes:** Teach the distinction concretely, because this is the one skill the session needs and most people do it wrong by default.

Walk the example on the slide. Four cards mention four different tools, but all four are the same underlying job: needing to trust a number without checking it three ways. Grouped by system you get four piles of one and no pattern. Grouped by job you get one cluster with weight behind it.

Give them the test question to use at their tables: what was the person actually trying to get done when this went wrong? If two cards answer that the same way, they belong together regardless of what tool is named on them.

---

### Slide 28: Table clustering round
**Timing:** 15:00

**Graphics:** A timer, and a simple diagram of the wall divided into table zones.

**On-slide words:**
> Your section of the wall. Group it. Fifteen minutes.

**Speaker notes:** Divide the wall into as many sections as there are tables, and assign each table a section. Everyone stands up and works at the wall, which is essential at this point in a long day.

Each table groups its section by job, physically moving cards. Loose groups are fine, and any card that does not fit anywhere goes in a holding area rather than being forced.

Two instructions that keep the output usable. Do not discard anything, including the odd ones, since a single card can be the sharpest signal on the wall. And notice duplication: if six cards say nearly the same thing, keep them all stacked together rather than consolidating, because the size of the stack is data.

**Facilitator note:** Table facilitators enforce grouping by job and interrupt gently whenever a table starts sorting by system name, which most will do at least once. Two-minute warning at the end.

---

### Slide 29: Consolidate the wall
**Timing:** 12:00

**Graphics:** Live. Point the camera at the wall if you have a feed, otherwise nothing on screen and all attention on the wall itself.

**On-slide words:**
> Now let's merge.

**Speaker notes:** The host and facilitators walk the whole wall with the room watching, merging groups that are really the same job across different table sections. Say your reasoning out loud as you move things, and invite challenge constantly.

Expect and welcome disagreement about where a card belongs. Resolve it by asking whoever wrote it, if they are willing to identify themselves, since the author's answer settles it faster than any debate.

Aim to land at somewhere between four and seven substantial clusters plus a holding area. Fewer than four and the statements will be too broad to design against tomorrow. More than seven and you have more clusters than tables.

---

### Slide 30: Name them
**Timing:** 8:00

**Graphics:** Blank sticky note headers ready to go above each cluster. Left empty on purpose.

**On-slide words:**
> What is this one really about?

**Speaker notes:** Go cluster by cluster and ask the room to name each one in plain language, in the words they would actually use with a colleague. Write their words verbatim, resisting every instinct to tidy the phrasing into something more professional. The rough version is the one they will recognize tomorrow.

Push back on any name that is just a system name or a department. Ask again: what is this one really about?

---

### Slide 31: Reframe into How Might We
**Timing:** 10:00

**Graphics:** The format shown once, cleanly, with one worked transformation from a complaint into an invitation.

**On-slide words:**
> How might we ______ ?

**Speaker notes:** Teach the format briefly and explain the logic rather than just the syntax. A complaint closes down and points at what is broken. A "how might we" opens up and points at what could be different, which is what a design brief needs to do.

Show the transformation on the slide. From "the reporting tool is terrible and I do not trust the numbers" to "how might we help someone trust a number without checking it three separate ways."

Do the first cluster together, out loud, with the room offering wording. Then send tables to draft one each for the remaining clusters, five minutes, and take them back one at a time. Refine live on the poster where needed, always keeping their vocabulary.

Watch for two failure modes. A statement with the solution already baked in, which forecloses tomorrow's studio and needs pushing back up a level. And a statement so broad it could mean anything, which needs narrowing until it names a real person doing a real thing.

**Facilitator note:** Write each final statement on a large poster as it is agreed. These posters are the physical objects that go on the tables tomorrow morning, so write them large and legibly.

---

### Slide 32: Confirm and lock
**Timing:** 5:00

**Graphics:** All the final How Might We posters, lined up together.

**On-slide words:**
> Tomorrow's agenda. Written by you.

**Speaker notes:** Read all of them back to the room, slowly, one at a time. Then ask two questions and genuinely wait for answers, since silence here often means people are too tired to speak rather than that they agree. Ask whether anything on the wall got lost, and whether anything big is missing entirely.

Make one change live if a real one is offered, even a small one, because visible responsiveness at this moment is worth more than a perfect set of statements.

Then close it: these are the problems the room will design against tomorrow morning, and every card that did not make a cluster is kept and goes into the record.

# CLOSE OF DAY 1 (10 min)

### Slide 33: What happens tonight and tomorrow
**Timing:** 6:00

**Graphics:** A simple two-column view of tomorrow's blocks, plus a photo of today's wall.

**On-slide words:**
> Tomorrow you design against these.

**Speaker notes:** Tell them exactly what happens next. Tonight the statements get printed onto table posters and the cards get transcribed so nothing is lost. Engineering leads receive the statements this evening so they arrive tomorrow having already thought about them.

Set tomorrow's expectations concretely, especially the physical ones. They will be working rather than watching, they will be asked to draw and no skill is required, and they will get honest feasibility feedback in the afternoon from the same engineering leads they heard from this morning.

Give the practical details: start time, dress, and to bring nothing but themselves.

---

### Slide 34: Close
**Timing:** 4:00

**Graphics:** The friction wall, full, photographed well. Nothing else.

**On-slide words:**
> Thank you.

**Speaker notes:** Keep this genuinely short. Two things, then stop.

Thank them specifically, naming one or two moments from the day rather than thanking the room generically. Specificity is what makes gratitude credible.

Then land the line the day was built for: they walked in this morning with friction they had been carrying privately, and they are walking out having named it collectively and turned it into tomorrow's agenda. Nobody handed them a plan. They wrote one.

Leave the wall up overnight if the venue allows. People will come back to it in the morning, and some of the best cards get written on day two.

## DESIGN NOTES FOR THE DECK

- Day 1 is cooler and more serious in tone than Day 2. Day 2 shifts to warm, hands, paper, and marker ink. The visual change should be felt when they walk in tomorrow.
- The friction wall recurs as a photograph on Slides 5, 19, 20, 25, 26, and 34. Use the actual current photo each time so the room watches it fill across the day. This is the strongest continuity device available and it costs almost nothing.
- Blocks 1, 2, and 6 are working sessions and should carry almost no text on screen. During working time the slide is a timer and an instruction.
- Block 3 is the existing deck and keeps its own visual language, including the iceberg, which then returns on Day 2's closing slide.
- Print the card format examples as table tents. People forget the format within twenty minutes and the tent saves the wall keeper from repeating it all day.
